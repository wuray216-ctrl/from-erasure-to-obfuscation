# From Erasure to Obfuscation

**A Paradigm Shift in Privacy in the Age of Generative AI**

A working paper proposing **narrative autonomy** as a new privacy strategy fitted to the era of large language models.

---

## What this paper argues

The "erasure paradigm" that has dominated privacy law for two decades — the right to be forgotten under GDPR, the right to deletion under China's PIPL — is hitting its limits. As generative AI replaces search engines as the primary way the public learns about individuals, the threat is no longer that *data exists* but that *data is automatically integrated into a stable, fluent profile*.

This paper proposes a complementary strategy: **narrative autonomy**. Individuals have a privacy interest in publishing multiple, internally inconsistent self-narratives that weaken the statistical coherence on which automated profiling depends — without deleting any data, and operating entirely under their own name.

The paper situates this concept against five adjacent privacy traditions (presentation of self, context collapse, obscurity, contextual integrity, obfuscation) and identifies its specific contribution: resistance directed at **automated integration**, not at data collection or audience management.

A speculative observation closes the argument: **model collapse** in generative AI may already be producing, as a side-effect, the kind of profile instability that ordinary individuals would have a privacy interest in cultivating deliberately.

## Key concepts proposed

- **Narrative autonomy** — a privacy interest in publishing multiple inconsistent self-narratives to resist algorithmic profile consolidation
- **Statistical coherence** — distinct from accuracy; the property by which generative output appears reliable due to internal consistency across runs, regardless of truth
- **Model collapse as accidental privacy infrastructure** — a hypothesis that recursive AI training degradation may benefit low-profile individuals' privacy as a side-effect
